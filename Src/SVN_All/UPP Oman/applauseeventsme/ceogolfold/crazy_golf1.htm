CRAZY GOLF & HOW IT ALL STARTED

 

Crazy Golf, is also known as mini-golf, minigolf, midget golf, goofy golf, extreme golf, or miniature golf. It is a game modelled after the sport of golf. The term putt-putt is a commonly used to describe the sport of miniature golf.

As in golf courses are commonly nine or eighteen holes long and the object is to hit the golf ball into the appropriate hole in the fewest strokes similarly in the Crazy Golf version, the only difference is that holes are significantly shorter and mostly consist of a putting surface but the objective remains the same. The game is made challenging by an arrangement of obstacles, ramps, and bunkers. It is a popular pastime in many areas of the world, especially among families.

The first Crazy golf course is often misattributed to one of two epochs:
• The 1916 backyard Thistle Dhu ("This'll Do") course in Pinehurst, North Carolina, USA.
• The 1927 Tom Thumb patent of Garnet Carter from Lookout Mountain, Tennessee, USA.

Neither of these courses—though each revolutionary in its own right—was the true "first" crazy golf course. The Ladies' Putting Club of St. Andrews, Scotland was the first crazy golf course, formed in 1867. Then, it was unacceptable for women to swing a club past their shoulders, so a crazy golf version of regulation golf was adapted for women to play.

Crazy golf rose to popularity in the late 1910s and early 1920s as a way for early golf fanatics to replicate major golf courses on a small scale. The game was commonly called "garden golf," and was played with a putter on grass. Thomas McCulloch Fairbairn, a golf fanatic, revolutionised the game in 1922 with his formulation of a suitable artificial green—a mixture of cottonseed hulls, sand, oil, and dye. With this discovery, crazy golf became accessible everywhere; by the late1920s there were over 150 rooftop courses in New York City alone.
