Grip

A Proper grip is the foundation of a good golf swing. A good grip will keep the club head square at impact and keep the shaft from turning in your hands. Pictured is the popular overlap grip.

Palms face each other. The little finger of the right hand overlaps the index finger of the left hand. Try for a relaxed and comfortable feel. A tight grip will hinder your rhythm and release.

Turning hands counter-clockwise on the shaft (weak grip), promotes a left-to-right flight of the ball. Turning hands clockwise (strong grip) promotes a right-to-left flight of the ball.

Stance

A square set-up will encourage a square impact. A square stance means that the feet are parallel to the target line. Imagine railroad tracks. Your feet are touching one rail, the ball is on the opposite rail.

A closed stance will encourage an inside to outside swingpath, causing a draw. An open stance will encourage an outside to inside swingpath which will tend to cause a slice or fade.

Position the ball inside of the left heel for all shots. This consistent position helps you develop a repeatable swing. Advanced players move the ball slightly back for shorter irons and specially for approach shots.

Swingpath

1) An Inside to square swingpath will generally result in a straight shot. (2) An inside to outside swingpath encourages a hook. (3) An outside to inside swingpath encourages a slice.

The target line is an imaginary line extending through the ball to the target. Your goal is a swingpath that starts on the inside striking the ball while moving slightly to the outside.

1) Clubface square to ball.
2) Clubface open to ball encourages a slice or face.
3) Clubface closed to ball encourages a hook.

Swing Basics

Weight shift two step

To hit the long ball you have to shift your weight properly. Improper weight shift is the major cause of lack of distance. Assume a narrow stance using a mid-iron. Take several practice swings. Begin your backswing by lifting your left foot slightly off the ground.

As you initiate your downswing replace your left foot and then lift your right foot as your follow-through. Trouble with this drill may indicate a weight transfer problem. You will know immediately if you are not shifting your weight back and then forward properly.

Release Positions

This drill produces an action called the release (rolling over of the hands and forearms). A proper release greatly helps generate increased clubhead speed. Put a tee in the butt end of the grip on a mid-iron. Begin a slow backswing. Stop when the tee in the grip points directly at the ball.

Swing through slowly until the tee in the grip again points at the starting ball position. Take some practice swings. Gradually increase the speed of your swing making sure to go on further back or further forward than your original position. Now hit some balls.

THE VARIOUS SWING SHOTS IN GOLF

         

 

A slice is a big ol' curve to the right, and is one of the problems that recreational golfers struggle with most. With a slice, the ball often starts out left of the target line before sweeping back right and winding up well right of the target.

  

Hook

A hook is the opposite of a slice; the ball curves greatly to the left. The ball often begins right of the target line (as in the illustration) before sweeping back to the left and winding up well left of the target.
         

Push

A push ball flight is one in which the ball starts out to the right of the target line and continues travelling right in a straight line (no additional curve, as with a slice), finishing well right of the target. The divot will also point to the right.

  

Pull

A pull is the opposite of a push. The ball starts out flying left of the target line and continues travelling left in a straight line (no additional curve, as with a hook), finishing well left of the target. The divot will also point to the left.
         

Fade

With a fade, the ball curves gently from left-to-right, moving toward the target after starting out left of the target line. The fade is a great shot to be able to play on command in order to better attack a pin or fairway, or to get around hazards.

  

Draw

A draw is the opposite of a fade. With a draw, the ball curves gently from right-to-left, moving toward the target after starting out right of the target line. A draw is a great shot to be able to play on command in order to better attack a pin or fairway, or to get around hazards. A controlled draw can also add yards to drives, producing additional roll.

Chipping Stroke

The chipping motion is similar to the putting stroke. Keep weight left, wrists firm and in front of clubhead. The goal is to get the ball on the green as soon as possible, tolling toward the hole.
 
With the ball back in your stance take a short backswing with no wrist movement. Make a descending blow and brush the ball from the turf. Maintain flex in your knees at all times.

The follow through should always be longer than the backswing. The stroke is dominated by the left arm. The right hand/forearm does not cross or roll over the left.

Putting

Take the stance with your eyes over the ball. Your shoulder and putter face should be square to the target line. The ball should be positioned off your left toe or instep.

Your stance should be steady with your weight favouring your left side. Begin your stroke with a low and slow take away. Follow through down the line towards your intermediate target.

Accelerate your stroke through the ball. Deceleration is the major cause of missed putts. A shortened backswing will give you a more accelerating stroke and follow-through.

“Perfect” Putting

Watch the great putters. They have differences in style, but there are certain key traits they share. Learning these traits and integrating them into your game through practice will give you a head start toward developing your own successful putting stroke.

The best place to watch good putters is in their natural habitation — the practice green. Notice how they view the green from different angles to judge break and grain. Observe how they set up to putt the same way each time. This is all part of their standard pre-putt routine.

Good putters share the follow traits:

• Control distance by the length of their stroke mostly on arm and shoulder movement
• Shoulders square to the target
• Putter Blade Square and vertical to the ball, focus on an intermediate target spot
• Long follow-through toward the hole.

Sand Play

Sand Shot

Dig your feet in for firm footing. Choke down on the club to compensate for feet being lower in sand. Open the clubface and body toward the target to encourage a full turn and follow-through.

You want to take a divot in the sand. Focus on where the club will enter the sand, not on the ball. Take a long shallow divot about 10 inches long. The finish of the follow through should be high. The more open the face, the higher the ball will pop-up. With practice, the normal bunker shot can become one of your more consistent stroke saving shots.
